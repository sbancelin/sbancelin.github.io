Standard SHG microscopy shows *where* fibrillar collagen is, but two complementary measurements reveal *how* it is organised. **Polarization-resolved SHG** [(Stoller et al., 2002)](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0006-3495(02)75673-7) records the signal while the incident polarization is rotated: the resulting anisotropy encodes the in-plane orientation and the degree of alignment of the fibrils. To go further and recover the **polarity** — the actual direction in which fibrils point, which ordinary SHG cannot distinguish — one has to measure the **phase** of the second-harmonic field. Because SHG is a coherent process, two fibrils pointing in opposite directions emit fields in phase opposition (a π shift), so a phase measurement maps fibril polarity directly.

**Interferometric SHG (I-SHG)** [(Rivard et al., 2013)](https://doi.org/10.1002/jbio.201300036) accesses this phase by letting the SHG from the sample interfere with a reference second-harmonic field generated by a quartz plate; a glass plate finely tunes the relative delay, and the interference fringes recorded while scanning the delay give the local SHG phase pixel by pixel. Applied to **cartilage**, I-SHG resolves domains of opposite collagen polarity that appear as a clear π phase shift in the phase map — and as two peaks in the phase histogram — information that is completely hidden in the intensity image.

The same principle extends beyond collagen: microtubules are also non-centrosymmetric and SHG-active. Using I-SHG on **mitotic spindles in living embryos**, we could follow the polarity of microtubule bundles in real time during cell division, distinguishing the two antiparallel halves of the spindle through their relative phase.
