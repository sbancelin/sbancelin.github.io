<div class="row align-items-center">
  <div class="col-md-7">
    <p>Standard SHG microscopy shows <em>where</em> fibrillar collagen is, but two complementary measurements reveal <em>how</em> it is organised. <strong>Polarization-resolved SHG</strong> records the signal while the incident polarization is rotated: the resulting anisotropy encodes the in-plane orientation and the degree of alignment of the fibrils. To go further and recover the <strong>polarity</strong> — the actual direction in which fibrils point, which ordinary SHG cannot distinguish — one has to measure the <strong>phase</strong> of the second-harmonic field. Because SHG is a coherent process, two fibrils pointing in opposite directions emit fields in phase opposition (a π shift), so a phase measurement maps fibril polarity directly.</p>
  </div>

</div>

**Interferometric SHG (I-SHG)** accesses this phase by letting the SHG from the sample interfere with a reference second-harmonic field generated by a quartz plate; a glass plate finely tunes the relative delay, and the interference fringes recorded while scanning the delay give the local SHG phase pixel by pixel. Applied to **cartilage**, I-SHG resolves domains of opposite collagen polarity that appear as a clear π phase shift in the phase map — and as two peaks in the phase histogram — information that is completely hidden in the intensity image.

The same principle extends beyond collagen: microtubules are also non-centrosymmetric and SHG-active. Using I-SHG on **mitotic spindles in living embryos**, we could follow the polarity of microtubule bundles in real time during cell division, distinguishing the two antiparallel halves of the spindle through their relative phase.
